Laws of the Game 2021-22

The laws of the game

Every soccer match is governed by the Laws of the Game (LOTG). The Laws of the Game are the only rules of soccer set and written by Fédération Internationale de Football Association (FIFA).

Summary of the Laws

Law 1 - The Field of Play

  • The colour of artificial surfaces must be green.
  • The field of play must be rectangular.
  • It must be marked with continuous lines which must not be dangerous.
  • All lines must be of the same width, which must not be more than 12 cm (5 ins).
Top

Law 2 - The Ball

  • All balls must be spherical and made of suitable material.
  • All balls must be of a pressure equal to 0.6-1.1 atmosphere (600-1,100 g/cm²) at sea level (8.5-15.6 lbs/sq in).
  • All balls used in matches played in an official FIFA-sanctioned competition must bear the "FIFA Inspected" mark.
Top

Law 3 - The Players

  • A match is played by two teams, each with a maximum of eleven players; one must be the goalkeeper.
  • A match may not start or continue if either team has fewer than seven players.
  • The team captain has no special status or privileges but has a degree of responsibility for the behaviour of the team.
Top

Law 4 - The Players' Equipment

  • A player must not use equipment or wear anything that is dangerous.
  • All items of jewellery (necklaces, rings, bracelets, earrings, leather bands, rubber bands, etc.) are forbidden and must be removed.
  • Using tape to cover jewellery is not permitted.
  • The compulsory equipment of a player comprises: a shirt with sleeves, shorts, socks, shinguards and footwear.
  • Equipment must not have any political, religious or personal slogans, statements or images.
Top

Law 5 - The Referee

  • The referee enforces the Laws of the Game.
  • The referee controls the match in cooperation with the other match officials.
  • The referee acts as timekeeper, keeps a record of the match and provides the appropriate authorities with a match report, including information on disciplinary action and any other incidents that occurred before, during or after the match.
Top

Law 6 - The Other Match Officials

  • Other match officials will assist the referee in controlling the match.
  • The match officials operate under the direction of the referee.
  • The 'on-field' match officials assist the referee with inspecting the field of play, the balls and players' equipment (including if problems have been resolved) and maintaining records of time, goals, misconduct etc.
Top

Law 7 - The Duration of the Match

  • A match lasts for two equal halves of 45 minutes.
  • A match may only be reduced if agreed between the referee and the two teams before the start of the match and if in accordance with competition rules.
  • Players are entitled to an interval at half-time, not exceeding 15 minutes.
  • Allowance is made by the referee in each half for all time lost in that half through: substitutions, assessment and/or removal of injured players, wasting time, disciplinary sanctions, medical stoppages, delays relating to VAR 'checks' and 'reviews' or any other cause, including any significant delay to a restart (e.g. goal celebrations).
Top

Law 8 - The Start and Restart of Play

  • A kick-off starts both halves of a match, both halves of extra time and restarts play after a goal has been scored.
  • Free kicks (direct or indirect), penalty kicks, throw-ins, goal kicks and corner kicks are other restarts.
  • A dropped ball is the restart when the referee stops play and the Law does not require one of the above restarts.
Top

Law 9 - The Ball In and Out of Play

  • The ball is out of play when: a) it has wholly passed over the goal line or touchline on the ground or in the air, b) play has been stopped by the referee, c)it touches a match official, remains on the field of play and: a team starts a promising attack, or the ball goes directly into the goal, or the team in possession of the ball changes.
  • The ball is in play at all other times when it touches a match official and when it rebounds off a goalpost, crossbar or corner flagpost and remains on the field of play.
Top

Law 10 - Determining the Outcome of a Match

  • A goal is scored when the whole of the ball passes over the goal line, between the goalposts and under the crossbar.
Top

Law 11 - Offside

  • It is not an offence to be in an offside position.
  • A player is in an offside position if: a) any part of the head, body or feet is in the opponents' half (excluding the halfway line) and, b) any part of the head, body or feet is nearer to the opponents' goal line than both the ball and the second-last opponent.
Top

Law 12 - Fouls and Misconducts

  • Direct and indirect free kicks and penalty kicks can only be awarded for offences committed when the ball is in play.
  • A direct free kick is awarded in the following scenarios: charging, jumping at, kicking, pushing, striking (including head-butting), tackling, tripping an opponent, deliberately committing a handball offence, holding an opponent, impeding an opponent with contact, biting or spitting at someone, throwing an object at the ball, an opponent or a match official, or making contact with the ball with a held object.
Top

Law 13 - Free Kicks

  • Direct and indirect free kicks are awarded to the opposing team of a player, substitute, substituted or sent-off player, or team official guilty of an offence.
  • A free kick can be taken by lifting the ball with a foot or both feet simultaneously.
  • Feinting to take a free kick to confuse opponents is permitted as part of football.
Top

Law 14 - The Penalty Kick

  • A penalty kick is awarded if a player commits a direct free kick offence inside their penalty area or off the field.
  • A goal may be scored directly from a penalty kick.
Top

Law 15 - The Throw-in

  • A throw-in is awarded to the opponents of the player who last touched the ball when the whole of the ball passes over the touchline, on the ground or in the air.
  • A goal cannot be scored directly from a throw-in: a) if the ball enters the opponents' goal - a goal kick is awarded, b) if the ball enters the thrower's goal - a corner kick is awarded.
Top

Law 16 - The Goal Kick

  • A goal kick is awarded when the whole of the ball passes over the goal line, on the ground or in the air, having last touched a player of the attacking team, and a goal is not scored.
  • A goal may be scored directly from a goal kick, but only against the opposing team; if the ball directly enters the kicker's goal, a corner kick is awarded to the opponents.
  • The ball is in play when it is kicked and clearly moves.
  • Opponents must be outside the penalty area until the ball is in play.
Top

Law 17 - The Corner Kick

  • A corner kick is awarded when the whole of the ball passes over the goal line, on the ground or in the air, having last touched a player of the defending team, and a goal is not scored.
  • A goal may be scored directly from a corner kick, but only against the opposing team; if the ball directly enters the kicker's goal a corner kick is awarded to the opponents.
Top
